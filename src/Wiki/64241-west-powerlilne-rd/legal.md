## Question 1: What happens to a home when the owner dies without a will, has no children, and the only family members are 4 nieces and nephews (3 of whom are siblings)?

### Intestate Succession in Louisiana

When someone dies without a will, the estate is distributed under **intestate succession** laws. Louisiana, as a civil law state, has unique rules compared to other states.

**Who inherits:**

- With no spouse and no children, the estate passes to **collateral relatives** — siblings first, then their descendants (nieces/nephews) by representation.
- If the deceased had **living siblings**, those siblings inherit — not the nieces/nephews directly.
- If the deceased's siblings are **all deceased**, the nieces/nephews inherit by representation. The 3 who share a parent would split that parent's share equally; the 4th niece/nephew would receive a separate portion.

### What Happens to the Home

- The estate, including the home, goes through **probate** (called a *succession proceeding* in Louisiana).
- A court-appointed administrator manages the assets.
- Heirs inherit the property **subject to the existing mortgage** — the debt transfers with the asset.
- The actual inherited value is the **equity**: \$200,000 market value − \$90,000 mortgage = **\$110,000 in equity**.

### What Happens If Mortgage Payments Stop

| Timeline | What Happens |
| --- | --- |
| 30–90 days missed | Loan goes into default; servicer contacts the estate/heirs |
| 90–120 days missed | Lender begins foreclosure proceedings (judicial process in Louisiana) |
| Foreclosure completion | Home is sold at auction; lender recovers debt; remaining proceeds go to heirs |

> **Key risk:** Foreclosure costs, legal fees, and auction discounts can significantly erode the available equity.

## Question 2: What is the likely amount heirs might receive after foreclosure?

### Starting Point

|     | Amount |
| --- | --- |
| Market Value | \$200,000 |
| Mortgage Balance | \$90,000 |
| Gross Equity | **\$110,000** |

### Costs That Erode Equity in Foreclosure

**1\. Foreclosure Sale Discount**  
Auction/foreclosure sales typically close at 70–85% of market value, as investors and distressed buyers expect below-market pricing.

- Estimated sale price: **\$140,000–\$170,000** (vs. \$200,000 market value)
- Loss from discount: **\$30,000–\$60,000**

**2\. Lender Fees & Costs Added to Debt**

| Fee Type | Estimated Range |
| --- | --- |
| Attorney/legal fees | \$3,000–\$8,000 |
| Late fees & penalties | \$1,500–\$3,000 |
| Property inspections & notices | \$500–\$1,500 |
| Accrued unpaid interest | \$2,000–\$5,000 |
| **Total added to debt** | **~\$7,000–\$17,000** |

**Effective amount owed to lender at foreclosure: ~\$97,000–\$107,000**

### Realistic Net to Heirs After Foreclosure

| Scenario | Conservative | Moderate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Foreclosure sale price | \$140,000 | \$165,000 |
| Less: total owed to lender | \$107,000 | \$97,000 |
| **Net to all heirs** | **~\$33,000** | **~\$68,000** |
| **Per heir (split 4 ways)** | **~\$8,000** | **~\$17,000** |

## Comparison: Foreclosure vs. Controlled Sale

|     | Foreclosure | Controlled Sale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sale price | \$140,000–\$170,000 | \$190,000–\$200,000 |
| Agent commission | —   | ~\$10,000–\$12,000 |
| Closing costs & probate fees | —   | ~\$5,000–\$10,000 |
| Lender payoff | \$97,000–\$107,000 | \$90,000 |
| **Net to all heirs** | **~\$33,000–\$68,000** | **~\$78,000–\$95,000** |
| **Per heir (4 ways)** | **~\$8,000–\$17,000** | **~\$19,500–\$23,750** |

### The Real Cost of Doing Nothing

By allowing foreclosure instead of managing a proper sale, heirs could **collectively lose \$45,000–\$62,000** in value due to the auction discount and accumulated lender fees.

* * *

## Practical Options for Heirs

1.  **Continue paying the mortgage** while the estate is settled, then sell or keep the home.
2.  **List the home on the open market** through the estate and split the net proceeds.
3.  **One heir refinances** into their name if they wish to keep the property.
4.  **Allow foreclosure** — technically possible, but the worst financial outcome given the existing equity.

* * *

## Bottom Line

Given \$110,000 in gross equity, allowing foreclosure is a significant financial mistake. Even a modest effort to coordinate a traditional sale through the estate can yield heirs **two to three times more** than a foreclosure auction.

A **Louisiana probate/succession attorney** typically costs \$2,500–\$5,000 for a straightforward case — a small investment relative to the equity at stake. The family should open a succession proceeding as soon as possible and make informed decisions about the property before the mortgage falls further behind.
